# Iron Throne of Ashes — Player's Manual

*A digital board game for 1–4 players · about 30–45 minutes · "Save the world, or take it."*

---

## The world is ending, and you want the throne

A kingdom is dying. From the wastes at its edges, a tide of ash called the **Blight** is creeping inward,
burning the land permanently dead as it comes. It is driven by the **Shadowking** — a patient, gloating
power that wants the whole realm reduced to cinder, and that hunts, above all, whoever is winning.

You are one of up to four rival Warlords. You want the same thing every other Warlord wants: to rule what's
left when the smoke clears. The catch is that there may be nothing left to rule unless someone holds the
darkness back — and the moment you pull ahead of your rivals, the Shadowking turns its gaze on *you*.

That is the whole game, and it lives in a single decision you will make over and over: **the cards in your
hand can hold back the dark, or they can win you the kingdom, but never both.** Learning when to spend on
which is learning to play.

You can play alone against three computer Warlords, with friends, or any mix — empty seats are taken by the
AI. The dark, always, is run by the game.

---

## How to win (and how everyone loses)

Start with the simple version. There are two roads to victory and one shared catastrophe.

**The ordinary way to win is to own the most land when time runs out.** The game lasts a set number of
rounds (fourteen at most, usually it ends sooner as the world burns). When the final dawn comes, whoever
holds the most living territory is the winner. Land is power; a title with no land behind it is nothing.

**The bold way to win is to seize the throne itself.** At the center of the map sits the **Keystone** — the
heart of the kingdom and the very place the Blight is racing to destroy. If you can march onto it and *hold*
it through the dark's full fury for one round, you win outright, then and there. It is dangerous and dramatic
and rare, and we'll come back to it once you know the basics.

**And everyone can lose together.** If the Blight reaches the Keystone and ashes it, the kingdom dies — and
every Warlord loses, winner and laggard alike. The dark doesn't care who was ahead. This is why your
deadliest rivals are also, sometimes, the only allies you have: nobody wins a dead world.

So the question that hangs over every round is never just "how do I get ahead?" It is "how do I get ahead
*without letting the world burn down around me* — and without becoming the one the dark decides to hunt?"

## The shape of a round

Every round moves through four steps, always in the same order. Think of them as a single breath:
**the threat, the pledge, your actions, and the dawn.**

### First, the threat

The Shadowking tells you exactly what it is about to do. It names a target — usually whoever is leading —
and announces its strike before it lands. It also tells you which way it is "turning its hunger," toward the
leader's part of the map. Nothing is hidden. This warning is your chance to think, to bargain, and to decide
how much you're willing to spend stopping it.

### Second, the pledge — the heart of it all

Now the table answers the threat. Each strike has a **price** in cards — a number you must collectively
reach to stop it cold. Everyone may **pledge** cards from their hand toward that price.

This is not a vote, and it is not all-or-nothing. Pledging is **proportional**: if the table puts up half of
what's needed, the strike is half as bad. If you fall short, the rest gets through — the Blight advances, and
the dark's named target takes a wound. One player refusing to help can hurt, but can never veto the others.

Here is the twist that makes the pledge the soul of the game: **the leader's cards count for only half.**
The Warlord who is winning is exactly the one the dark is hunting, and exactly the one whose help is worth
least when defending the world. So you'll find the table quietly arguing every round — *you're ahead, you
pay* — and nobody especially wants to be the frontrunner too soon.

In the standard game everyone pledges in the open: you watch the numbers climb, you adjust, you hold your
nerve or blink first. It plays like a staredown. (In the hidden-traitor mode, pledges are secret — more on
that much later.)

A word of warning that will save you games: **doing nothing is not safe.** The Blight creeps a step closer
to the center every single dawn no matter what, so a Warlord who stops pledging to save their own cards is
quietly feeding their own defeat. Turtling doesn't buy a draw. It buys a slower loss for everyone.

### Third, your actions

Now you take your turn. Players act one at a time, in an order the game fixes and shows you. On your turn you
get **two actions** (only one if you've been beaten down into Broken Court — more on that later), and you
spend **Banners** — a separate, renewable kind of money — to move and to claim.

We'll list everything you can do in a moment. The essentials are: **march** a piece across the map, **claim**
the land you're standing on, **raid** a rival who shares your square, and **strike** at the dark's forces
when they're in reach.

### Fourth, the dawn

The round ends and the world turns. Your Banners reset and you collect fresh income from the land you hold.
You draw cards back up toward your hand limit. Any beaten Warlords check whether they've recovered. The
Blight makes its inevitable creep inward. And every so often, the Shadowking **escalates** — growing from a
distant menace into an active horror, changing the very rules of how it attacks. Then a new threat is
announced, and the next round begins.

## Your two kinds of strength: Cards and Banners

It's worth understanding why the game gives you two different resources, because the difference is the
difference between your two ambitions.

**Cards are your strategic strength.** You hold a small hand of them — you start with four, and can hold up
to six. Cards are how you pledge against the dark, how you fuel your fights, and how you make a last
desperate stand. They carry over from round to round, so spending one now is a real sacrifice you'll feel
later. Cards are the world-saving, throne-taking currency, and there is never enough of them. This scarcity
*is* the game.

**Banners are your tactical strength.** You generate a fresh supply every dawn from the land you hold, and
they don't carry over — use them or lose them. Banners pay for marching and claiming. Holding a rich
**Forge** floods you with them. Banners are how you move and grow on the board this turn.

When you spend cards, you are trading away your grip on the dark and the long game. When you spend Banners,
you are just playing this turn's board. Keep the two straight and you'll always know what a decision really
costs you.

## The map: the Closing Ring

The kingdom is a ring of seventeen places, arranged in rings within rings, the same in every direction so
that no starting position is luckier than another. As the game goes on and the Blight burns inward, the
living board literally shrinks toward the center.

```
                 [Keep N]──[Hold]──[Keep E]
                   │   ╲              ╱   │
                [Forge NW]        [Forge NE]
                   │      ╲      ╱      │
              [Approach NW]─[Approach NE]
                   │      KEYSTONE      │
              [Approach SW]─[Approach SE]
                   │      ╱      ╲      │
                [Forge SW]        [Forge SE]
                   │   ╱              ╲   │
                 [Keep S]──[Hold]──[Keep W]
```

Your **Keep** is your home corner, claimed from the start. It can't be burned while you still stand, so it's
the one place you never have to worry about — until you're beaten.

The **Holdings** along the outer edges are the early prizes: easy land worth claiming in the opening
land-grab.

The four **Forges** in the middle belt are the real treasures. Each one pours out three times the income of
ordinary land, so whoever holds the Forges has the Banners to dominate the board. They're also gates: every
path from the outer ring to the center passes through a Forge, so when a rival marches through *your* Forge,
they pay you a toll. (Unless the two of you have sworn an oath — then they pass free.) Expect the Forges to
be the bloodiest ground on the map.

The four **Approaches** form the inner ring — the only roads to the center. They're chokepoints: an enemy
can't simply stroll through an Approach you're holding, they have to fight you for it.

And at the very center is the **Keystone**: the throne, and the doomsday. It's where the Blight is headed,
and it's the prize a daring Warlord can seize to win it all.

The Blight enters from the outer edges and eats its way inward along the spokes. Importantly, it doesn't
spread evenly — each round the Shadowking drives it harder down *one* direction, aimed at whoever is leading.
So there's always a visible, moving front of fire, and it leans toward the winner. Ash, once made, is
permanent, though you can still cross burned ground if you're willing to pay a little extra to march through
it.

## Everything you can do on your turn

Here is your full menu of actions. You won't use all of them every game, and the first handful are all you
need to start.

The bread and butter: **March** a piece one space (it costs a Banner, more to cross ash, plus a toll through
a rival's Forge). **Claim** the unclaimed land you're standing on for a Banner — though you can't claim a
square a Death Knight is sitting on; you'll have to kill it first. **Raid** a rival who shares your square,
or **Strike** one of the dark's forces. **Recruit** a new fighting piece to your retinue so you can act in
more than one place at once.

When the time comes to defend: if losing a fight would cost you a stronghold, you may make a **Last Stand** —
throw any number of extra cards into the fight for a do-or-die reversal. Win and you keep the ground and even
beat back the tide; lose and the land falls *and* those cards are gone, often the very ones you needed for
the next pledge. It's the biggest gamble in the game, and you decide how much to bet.

When you want to deal with another player: **Swear an Oath** with a rival — it's free, it's public, and while
it lasts neither of you can raid the other, you both pass each other's Forges toll-free, and you each collect
a little extra income. Honor it to the end and you're both rewarded; **Break** it (which costs you an action)
and you grab a quick windfall and your freedom to attack — but the dark will mark you as a betrayer and come
for you.

When a rival has fallen: **Rescue** a beaten ally next to you. It costs you a card, they pay you back in
Banners, and the act binds the two of you in an oath. It's a deal with strings, not a kindness.

And the patient, political path: **Parley**, which only a Herald can do — a way to push back the dark
without spending any cards at all. (Heralds are an advanced option; see below.)

When you're done, you **Pass**.

## When the fighting goes against you: Broken Court

Nobody is ever knocked out of this game. When a Warlord takes too many wounds — from rivals or from the dark
itself — they don't die. They **Break**: their shield cracks, and they fall into the Broken Court.

Being Broken is a humbling, but it has teeth. You act less (just one action a turn) but you *earn more* — a
comeback subsidy to help you claw your way back, though it shrinks the longer you stay down, so it's a ramp
out, not a comfortable place to live. Crucially, **you keep your full voice in the pledge**: being beaten
never silences you against the dark. You can still raid, still strike, still make a last stand. And the lands
you lost while Broken turn to ash and feed the Blight — so a Warlord who grinds a rival into the dirt is
helping the whole world burn a little faster.

You recover on your own after a couple of rounds, so no one can be held down forever as a hostage. And while
you're Broken you can't be counted as the leader — so don't imagine you can lose on purpose to dodge the
dark's attention. The game has thought of that.

## Why you don't want to be winning (yet)

This is the single most important idea to carry into your first game, so here it is plainly: **the leader is
the dark's prey.**

The Warlord holding the most land is the **Crown**, and the Crown is a target painted on a back. The
Shadowking hunts the Crown by name. The Crown's pledged cards count for only half, so the leader is also the
worst defender. Pull ahead, and you become the one the dark comes for *and* the one least able to stop it.

This is not a punishment for playing well — it's the engine of the whole game. It means you don't simply race
to the front and stay there. You time your lead. You let a rival hold the Crown and soak the dark's attention
while you build quietly. You make deals. And in the last rounds, you make your push for the front exactly
when it's too late for the dark to make you pay for it.

A good Warlord is rarely in first place. A good Warlord is in first place at the dawn of the final round.

## Knowing the dark: the Shadowking up close

The Shadowking is not a shuffled deck of random misfortunes. It is a character with a plan, and because it
always tells you that plan, you can play against it like an opponent rather than weather a storm.

It hunts deliberately. If someone has seized the throne, it goes for them. Otherwise it goes for whoever has
most earned its hatred — its **grudge** — and failing that, for the Crown. Every Warlord has a public grudge
meter, and here is the clever part: **you raise your grudge by hurting the dark.** Kill its Death Knights,
take back its Forges, and you've wounded it — but you've also drawn its eye. This means the table can goad
the villain into hunting a rival. It also means there's a beautiful trap built in: if you're *leading*,
hurting the dark makes it hunt you all the harder, but if you're *trailing*, you can fight the dark almost
freely. Striking at the darkness is a tool for the underdog and a danger for the frontrunner.

The dark remembers, too. Break an oath and your name jumps to the top of its list — the Shadowking is the
enforcer of every promise broken at the table.

And it grows. It begins as **the Whisper**, a distant dread that nibbles at the edges. As the land burns it
becomes **the March**, sending Death Knights raiding deep toward the throne. And at last it becomes **the
Reckoning** — a final act where it strikes everywhere at once and the end comes fast. Each stage doesn't just
hit harder; it changes what the dark is allowed to do. You'll feel the noose tighten.

Its main soldiers are the **Death Knights** — mobile threats that march toward the throne, block you from
claiming the squares they stand on, and serve the Blight. Killing one is one of the best plays in the whole
game: you take the square it died on as your own, you shove the Blight back down that spoke, and you've struck
a real blow. Hunt them when you safely can.

## The bold gambit: seizing the throne

Now that you know the shape of the game, here is the dramatic shortcut to victory.

If you march a force onto the **Keystone** at the center and hold it through the dawn, your **Crown's Gambit**
goes public — and the Shadowking names *you* as its sole target. Every rival now has a full round to tear you
off the throne: raid you, out-maneuver you, or simply stop pledging and let the dark pour its fury onto you.
Survive on the Keystone to the *next* dawn, and the kingdom is yours, instantly, no matter who held more land.

It is not free. While you sit the throne your pledged cards barely count, and your forces are pinned to the
center, unable to defend your homeland from the spreading ash — a failed gambit can cost you everything you
built. A wise Warlord only goes for it holding enough cards in reserve to weather that one terrible named
round. While you garrison the throne the dark can't burn it out from under you directly — instead it
savages the lands around you. The gambit risks *your* position, not the whole world.

It doesn't happen often — maybe one game in seven — but the mere *threat* of it shadows every negotiation.
Everyone at the table is always a little bit watching the center.

## When there's a traitor among you: Blood Pact mode

This is an optional way to play, and you should learn the ordinary game first. With Blood Pact switched on,
**one human player is secretly sworn to the dark.** The traitor wins if and only if the Blight ashes the
Keystone — the world ends — *and* nobody correctly unmasked them.

It changes the texture of everything. The dark burns hotter with a traitor at the table, so the apocalypse
becomes a real possibility, not a distant one. The traitor's whole problem is hiding: pledges are now secret,
revealed only as a table-wide total, so a thin pledge can vanish into the crowd. Every round the traitor must
choose between pledging enough to *look* loyal (which really does help the table — that's the price of the
disguise) and quietly starving the defense to hasten the end (the tell that can give them away). Pledging
*nothing* is what looks guilty; pledging merely a little is just ordinary thrift.

The loyal Warlords have tools to hunt the hidden one. You can study the **Suspicion Log** of who has been
pledging how much. You can spend Banners on an **Audit** to drag one rival's secret pledge into the light.
And when you're ready, you can call an **Accusation** — but every other living Warlord must agree to it, and
a wrong accusation costs the accusers dearly while vindicating the innocent and handing the real traitor
perfect cover. Be sure before you point the finger.

## A Warlord's first game: what to actually do

If this is your first time, don't try to hold every thread at once. Here's a way to think about it.

In the early rounds, **grab land** — claim the nearby Holdings, and fight for a Forge if you can, because the
Banners it brings will carry your whole game. Pledge a moderate amount against the dark; you don't have to
stop every strike, you just don't want to be the one who let a bad one through onto your own doorstep
(remember, the dark eats a contributor's land *last*).

In the middle rounds, **watch the Crown.** If you're out front, ease off — let a rival take the lead and the
dark's attention, and consider striking a Death Knight or two while you're safely in the pack. Swear an oath
with whoever you'd rather not fight, so you can both turn your strength elsewhere. Keep an eye on the front of
fire and which way it's leaning.

In the late rounds, **time your push.** As the Reckoning closes in and the final dawn approaches, that's when
you want to surge to the front — claiming, raiding, making your play for the lead exactly late enough that the
dark can't punish you for it before the clock runs out. And always, always keep a few cards in hand: the
strike you fail to blunt at the end could be the one that breaks you, or burns the world before anyone wins
it.

Above all, remember the one decision under all the others. Every card is a choice between the world and the
throne. Play that choice well — spend to survive when you must, hoard to strike when you can, and never let
your hunger for the crown blind you to the ash crawling toward the center — and you'll be the Warlord still
standing, on the most living ground, when the last dawn breaks.
